Case study · Packaging system

Whidbey Island Seafood Co.

A wild-caught Alaskan seafood brand, packaged across its whole catalog: smoked fish, seasonings, pâté, pet treats, and the boxes they ship in.

Client
Whidbey Island Seafood Co.
Role
Packaging & brand system
Year
2023–2024
Scope
Cartons · labels · jars · pet line · shipping

Overview

Whidbey Island Seafood Co. (“The People’s Fisherman”) sells wild-caught Alaskan seafood across a growing range of products, from smoked salmon and black cod to seasonings, pâté, and pet treats. The work here is the packaging system that holds all of it together: one identity stretched across cartons, pouches, jars, tubs, labels, and shipping boxes.

The brief

A seafood company that keeps adding products runs into a familiar problem: every new item is a chance for the brand to drift. Different printers, different pack formats, different constraints. Soon the shelf looks like a dozen companies instead of one. The goal was a kit of parts strong and flexible enough that any new product could be built quickly and still look unmistakably like Whidbey Island.

One system across the line

The smoked line shows the system at work. A shared layout (the crab mark, a hand-engraved fish, a bold species name, and a color band for the flavor) lets King Salmon, Sockeye, and Black Cod share a face while each variety stays easy to tell apart. Lemon Pepper is green, Cajun is red, Garlic Pepper is purple, Salmon Candy is blue; the color does the sorting so the layout doesn’t have to change.

A recognizable kit of parts

Underneath every product are the same building blocks: the orange crab monogram and the “People’s Fisherman” tagline, black-and-white fish engravings that nod to old fish-market prints, a deep navy anchored by a single warm orange, and a wave motif that carries across formats. Sustainability marks (MSC, Alaska Seafood, FishChoice) sit in a consistent lockup so the certifications read as part of the brand rather than clutter.

Built for the printer

The black cod cartons were drawn as full folding-carton dielines: every panel, flap, and glue tab in place, with the nutrition panel, allergen statement, and net weight set to spec. Designing on the flat means the artwork wraps correctly once the box is folded, and the flavor color carries around all four sides.

Beyond the shelf

The identity stretches past food, too. A wild-caught salmon pet-treat line borrows the same crab mark and wave pattern in a friendlier register, and the direct-to-consumer shipping box turns a plain kraft mailer into a brand moment: the arriving package feels like part of the product.

Down to the traceability label

Even the frozen traceability labels are part of the system. Harvester, harvest area, processor, dates, and net weight are laid out clearly for cold-chain and regulatory needs, in two label sizes, without losing the brand: the same care goes into the panel nobody frames and the render everybody sees.

Outcome

A packaging system that spans the whole catalog (smoked fish, seasonings, pâté, pet treats, frozen labels, and shipping) and gives Whidbey Island one voice on the shelf, however far the product line grows.
